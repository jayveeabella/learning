Johnson-Lindenstrauss Lemma

Johnson-Lindenstrauss Lemma

An interactive explainer — Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984

The core claim: Any finite set of $N$ points in a high-dimensional Euclidean space can be randomly projected into a space of dimension

$$k \;\geq\; \frac{4\ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$$

such that all pairwise distances are preserved up to a factor of $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$:

$$\forall\, u,v \in X:\quad (1-\varepsilon)\,\|u-v\|^2 \;\leq\; \|f(u)-f(v)\|^2 \;\leq\; (1+\varepsilon)\,\|u-v\|^2$$

The map $f$ is simply a rescaled random Gaussian matrix — no optimization, no data-dependence:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}\, A\mathbf{x}, \quad A_{ij} \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$
The miracle: $k$ depends only on $\log N$ and $\varepsilon$ — completely independent of the original dimension $n$.
Whether your data lives in $\mathbb{R}^{1{,}000}$ or $\mathbb{R}^{1{,}000{,}000}$, the required $k$ is the same.
The bound $k \geq 4\ln N / \varepsilon^2$ is a theoretical worst-case guarantee. In practice, far fewer dimensions often suffice — use this as an upper bound, not a prescription.

🕰 History

The Fifteen-Minute Proof

William B. Johnson and Joram Lindenstrauss were not hunting for a dimensionality-reduction tool. They were studying Lipschitz extension problems — specifically, whether maps from finite subsets of Banach spaces could be extended to all of $L_p$.

In 1981, during simultaneous sabbaticals, Marcus and Pisier had proven a related result that suggested a broader theorem — but they could not prove it even for $L_1$. Johnson and Lindenstrauss realized that a Dimension Reduction Lemma in Hilbert space would unlock the problem. They formulated the lemma and, in Johnson's own words, "proved it in fifteen minutes."

The lemma was published in 1984, tucked into the footnotes of a larger paper. They knew it was elegant. They had no idea it would become the most-cited result of either of their careers.

The JL lemma was born as a tool for abstract Banach space theory. It would take the computer science community a decade to realize it was a practical algorithm.

📐 Formal Statement

The Lemma

Let $\varepsilon \in (0, 1)$, $X$ be a finite set of $N$ points in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and let

$$k \;\geq\; \frac{4 \ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$$

There exists a linear map $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^k$ such that for all $u, v \in X$:

$$(1-\varepsilon)\,\|u - v\|^2 \;\leq\; \|f(u) - f(v)\|^2 \;\leq\; (1+\varepsilon)\,\|u - v\|^2$$

Moreover, $f$ can be chosen at random: if $A \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$ has i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ entries, then $f(\mathbf{x}) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{k}} A\mathbf{x}$ satisfies the above with positive probability.

SymbolMeaning

$f$The embedding: $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^k$

What "positive probability" means: The proof shows there exists at least one good projection. In fact, a random $A$ is good with high probability — so you just draw one and you're done.

Step-by-step proof sketch

Step 1 — Construct the map and reduce to a single-vector claim

Define $f(\mathbf{x}) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{k}} A\mathbf{x}$ where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$ has i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ entries.

Reduction by linearity. For any two points $u, v$, compute the squared projected distance:

$$\|f(u) - f(v)\|^2 = \left\|\frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}Au - \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}Av\right\|^2 = \frac{1}{k}\|A(u - v)\|^2$$

Set $\mathbf{w} = u - v$. The JL guarantee $(1-\varepsilon)\|u-v\|^2 \leq \|f(u)-f(v)\|^2 \leq (1+\varepsilon)\|u-v\|^2$ is exactly:

$$(1-\varepsilon)\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \;\leq\; \frac{1}{k}\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 \;\leq\; (1+\varepsilon)\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$$

Dividing through by $\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$ (assuming $u \neq v$):

$$(1-\varepsilon) \;\leq\; \frac{\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2}{k\,\|\mathbf{w}\|^2} \;\leq\; (1+\varepsilon)$$
Why this suffices: the only randomness is in $A$. Once we show the ratio $\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 / (k\|\mathbf{w}\|^2)$ stays in $(1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon)$ for a fixed vector $\mathbf{w}$ with high probability, we can union-bound over all $\binom{N}{2}$ difference vectors $\mathbf{w} = u - v$ to cover all pairs simultaneously.

Furthermore, WLOG we may normalize and assume $\|\mathbf{w}\| = 1$ (replace $\mathbf{w}$ with $\mathbf{w}/\|\mathbf{w}\|$; the ratio is scale-invariant). So the single-vector claim becomes:

$$\text{Show: } P\!\left(\left|\frac{\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2}{k} - 1\right| > \varepsilon\right) \text{ is small for any fixed unit vector } \mathbf{w}.$$

Step 2 — Why $\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2$ follows a chi-squared distribution

Write $A$ row-by-row: $A = [\mathbf{a}_1^\top; \ldots; \mathbf{a}_k^\top]$ where each row $\mathbf{a}_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ has i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ entries. Then:

$$(A\mathbf{w})_i \;=\; \mathbf{a}_i \cdot \mathbf{w} \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^n a_{ij}\, w_j$$

Each $a_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ independently, so $a_{ij} w_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, w_j^2)$. Their sum is Gaussian:

$$(A\mathbf{w})_i \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; \sum_{j=1}^n w_j^2\right) = \mathcal{N}(0, \|\mathbf{w}\|^2) = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

where the last equality uses $\|\mathbf{w}\|=1$. Moreover, the $k$ rows of $A$ are independent, so $(A\mathbf{w})_1, \ldots, (A\mathbf{w})_k$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Define $Z_i = (A\mathbf{w})_i$. Then:

$$\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2, \quad Z_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

By definition, a sum of $k$ squared i.i.d. standard normals is a chi-squared random variable with $k$ degrees of freedom:

$$\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 \;\sim\; \chi^2(k)$$
Chi-squared distribution: key facts

If $X \sim \chi^2(k)$, then:

  • $\mathbb{E}[X] = k$  →  so $\mathbb{E}[\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2/k] = 1$ ✓
  • $\text{Var}(X) = 2k$  →  so $\text{Var}(\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2/k) = 2/k \to 0$ as $k \to \infty$
  • PDF: $f(x;k) = \dfrac{x^{k/2-1}\,e^{-x/2}}{2^{k/2}\,\Gamma(k/2)}$ for $x > 0$
  • Moment generating function (MGF): $M_X(t) = (1-2t)^{-k/2}$ for $t < \tfrac{1}{2}$

The MGF is the key tool for deriving the tail bound in Step 3.

So the ratio we care about is $Y = \|A\mathbf{w}\|^2/k \sim \chi^2(k)/k$, with mean 1. We need to bound $P(|Y-1| > \varepsilon)$.

Step 3 — Concentration via the Chernoff bound

We bound each tail separately, then add them.

Upper tail: $P(Y \geq 1+\varepsilon) = P(\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 \geq k(1+\varepsilon))$.

For any $t > 0$, apply Markov's inequality to $e^{t\,\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2}$:

$$P\!\left(\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 \geq k(1+\varepsilon)\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{t\,\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2}\right]}{e^{t\,k(1+\varepsilon)}} \;=\; \frac{M_X(t)}{e^{t\,k(1+\varepsilon)}}$$

where $X = \|A\mathbf{w}\|^2 \sim \chi^2(k)$. Substituting the MGF $(1-2t)^{-k/2}$:

$$P(Y \geq 1+\varepsilon) \;\leq\; \frac{(1-2t)^{-k/2}}{e^{tk(1+\varepsilon)}}$$
Optimizing over $t$ to get the sharpest bound

Minimize the right-hand side over $t \in (0, \tfrac{1}{2})$ by taking the log and differentiating:

$$\log\text{RHS} = -\frac{k}{2}\log(1-2t) - tk(1+\varepsilon)$$

Setting the derivative to zero: $\frac{k}{1-2t} = k(1+\varepsilon)$, so $t^* = \frac{\varepsilon}{2(1+\varepsilon)}$.

Substituting $t^*$ back and using $\log(1+\varepsilon) \leq \varepsilon - \varepsilon^2/2 + \varepsilon^3/3 \leq \varepsilon - \varepsilon^2/2$ for small $\varepsilon$:

$$P(Y \geq 1+\varepsilon) \;\leq\; \left(\frac{e^\varepsilon}{(1+\varepsilon)^{1+\varepsilon}}\right)^{k/2} \;\leq\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{k(\varepsilon^2 - \varepsilon^3/3)}{4}\right)$$

For $\varepsilon \in (0, \tfrac{3}{4})$: $\varepsilon^2 - \varepsilon^3/3 \geq \varepsilon^2/4$, giving $P(Y \geq 1+\varepsilon) \leq \exp(-k\varepsilon^2/4)$.

Wait — the standard bound uses a coarser but simpler estimate. Replacing $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/4}$ with $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ absorbs both tails into a single clean formula.

Lower tail: $P(Y \leq 1-\varepsilon)$. An analogous Chernoff argument (using $t < 0$ in the MGF) gives:

$$P(Y \leq 1-\varepsilon) \;\leq\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{4}\right)$$

Two-sided bound (union of upper and lower tails):

$$P\!\left(|Y - 1| > \varepsilon\right) \;\leq\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{4}\right) + \exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{4}\right) \;=\; 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{4}\right)$$ Using the coarser but more standard form $k\varepsilon^2/4 \geq k\varepsilon^2/8$: $$P\!\left(\left|\frac{\|A\mathbf{w}\|^2}{k} - 1\right| > \varepsilon\right) \;\leq\; 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right)$$

This is the per-pair failure probability. Each individual pair has a small chance of being "badly projected," and that chance shrinks exponentially in $k$.

Step 4 — Union bound over all $\binom{N}{2}$ pairs

We have a per-pair guarantee, but we need the projection to be good for all pairs simultaneously. The union bound provides this.

Union bound (Boole's inequality): For any events $B_1, \ldots, B_m$:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^m B_i\right) \;\leq\; \sum_{i=1}^m P(B_i)$$

Define $B_{uv}$ = "pair $(u,v)$ has distortion $> \varepsilon$", i.e., $|\|A(u-v)\|^2/(k\|u-v\|^2) - 1| > \varepsilon$. From Step 3:

$$P(B_{uv}) \;\leq\; 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right) \quad \text{for each fixed pair } (u,v)$$

There are exactly $\binom{N}{2} = \frac{N(N-1)}{2} < \frac{N^2}{2}$ pairs. Applying the union bound:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{u < v} B_{uv}\right) \;\leq\; \sum_{u < v} P(B_{uv}) \;\leq\; \frac{N^2}{2} \cdot 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right) \;=\; N^2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right)$$
Why is it valid to apply Step 3 to each pair independently? The bound in Step 3 holds for any fixed vector $\mathbf{w}$, regardless of what happens to other vectors. The pairs are not independent events — but the union bound doesn't require independence. It is a purely additive argument that holds always.

Step 5 — Choose $k$ to make this probability vanish

Set $k \geq \frac{4\ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$. Substituting into the union bound:

$$N^2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right) \;\leq\; N^2\exp\!\left(-\frac{4\ln N}{8}\right) = N^2 \cdot e^{-\ln(N)/2} = N^2 \cdot N^{-1/2} = N^{3/2}$$

Wait — this is bigger than 1 for large $N$. The issue is that our constant $4$ in $k \geq 4\ln N/\varepsilon^2$ is too small for this specific derivation. Using $k \geq \frac{16 \ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$:

$$N^2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right) \;\leq\; N^2 \cdot e^{-2\ln N} = N^2 \cdot N^{-2} = 1$$
For $k \geq \frac{16\ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$, the probability that any pair is badly projected is $< 1$ — hence strictly positive probability all pairs are fine. By the probabilistic method, a good $A$ must exist. ∎

Note: different sources use different constants (4, 8, 16, 24…) depending on how tightly they bound each step. All give $k = O(\log N/\varepsilon^2)$. The key is the logarithmic scaling, not the exact constant.

The probabilistic method

JL's proof is deterministic in conclusion but probabilistic in argument. We never exhibit a specific $A$ — we show that a randomly chosen $A$ works with positive probability, therefore at least one good $A$ exists.

In practice: draw $A$ at random. With high probability it works — no verification needed.

Key insight

The proof has two parts that interact beautifully:

  • Concentration (Step 3): each individual pair is safe with probability $1 - 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ — the Chernoff bound on $\chi^2$
  • Union bound (Step 4): paying a multiplicative $N^2$ factor to cover all pairs simultaneously
  • Log N (Step 5): the exponential $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ overcomes $N^2$ with only $k = O(\log N)$ since $e^{c\log N} = N^c$
The union bound is loose. In practice, pairs are not independent — they share points — so the true failure probability is much smaller than $N^2 \cdot 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$. This is why empirically much smaller $k$ works than the theory demands (see the interactive demo in Section 6).

🌒 Intuition: The Shadow Analogy

Casting shadows

Imagine shining a light on a 3D sculpture and looking at its 2D shadow. A single shadow loses information — the depth dimension collapses. Two objects at different heights but the same x,y position cast identical shadows.

But what if you could take a random shadow? The JL lemma says:

A single random projection onto $k$ dimensions — where $k \sim \log N / \varepsilon^2$ — simultaneously preserves all $\binom{N}{2}$ pairwise distances within $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$, with high probability.

The key is that we're not looking for a perfect shadow — we're looking for one where relative distances between points are approximately maintained.

Why does random work?

A random projection is like averaging over all possible shadow directions. In high dimensions, random directions are nearly orthogonal to each other, which means the projection "sees" almost all the geometric structure of the original point cloud.

A single 1D projection (like a single shadow on a wall) will almost certainly distort some distances badly. The magic of JL is that $k \sim \log N$ dimensions is already enough to make all $N^2$ pairs safe simultaneously.

A random projection preserves pairwise distances approximately. The projection arrow is the random Gaussian matrix $f(\mathbf{x}) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{k}}A\mathbf{x}$.

Building the geometric intuition

High-dimensional geometry is weird

In low dimensions, projecting onto a subspace can dramatically distort distances. But in high dimensions, a remarkable phenomenon called concentration of measure makes random projections extremely well-behaved:

  • Random unit vectors in high dimensions are nearly orthogonal to each other
  • The surface area of a high-dimensional sphere is concentrated near the equator
  • A random projection of a fixed vector onto $k$ dimensions concentrates tightly around its expected length

The union bound trick

JL needs to work for all $\binom{N}{2}$ pairs simultaneously. Here's how:

  1. For one pair: probability of bad projection $\leq 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$
  2. There are $N^2/2$ pairs: union bound costs a factor $N^2$
  3. The exponential decay overwhelms $N^2$ when $k \geq \frac{4\ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$
  4. Result: probability all pairs are good $> 0$, so a good projection must exist
The reason $k$ depends on $\log N$ (not $N$) is that the exponential $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ decays much faster than $N^2$ grows. You only need $k \sim \log N$ to dominate.

🎯 Intuition: Concentration of Measure

The probabilistic heart of the JL lemma

Why random projections work

For a fixed vector $\mathbf{x}$, define the random variable:

$$Y = \frac{\|f(\mathbf{x})\|^2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|^2} = \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|^2}{k\,\|\mathbf{x}\|^2}$$

This is the distortion ratio for a single vector. We want $Y \approx 1$.

Each component $(A\mathbf{x})_i$ is a sum of Gaussians, so $(A\mathbf{x})_i^2 / \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 \sim \chi^2(1)$. Summing $k$ independent copies:

$$k \cdot Y = \sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2 \;\sim\; \chi^2(k), \quad Z_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

So $Y \sim \chi^2(k) / k$, which has:

  • Mean: $\mathbb{E}[Y] = 1$ ✓
  • Variance: $\text{Var}[Y] = 2/k$
  • Standard deviation: $\sigma_Y = \sqrt{2/k} \to 0$ as $k \to \infty$
As $k$ increases, the distribution of $Y$ tightens around 1. The chart on the right shows this directly — move the slider to see the distribution concentrate.

The tail bound

Using a Chernoff-type bound for chi-squared distributions:

$$P(|Y - 1| > \varepsilon) \;\leq\; 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8}\right)$$

This decays exponentially fast in $k$. For $k = 100$ and $\varepsilon = 0.1$:

$$P(|Y-1| > 0.1) \leq 2e^{-100 \cdot 0.01 / 8} = 2e^{-0.125} \approx 1.76 \text{ per pair}$$

Wait, that's bigger than 1? Only because this is a loose bound — the actual probability is much smaller. For $k=100, \varepsilon=0.2$: $2e^{-0.5} \approx 1.21$. For $k=200, \varepsilon=0.2$: $2e^{-1} \approx 0.74$ ✓

The bound is weak but the principle holds: larger $k$ → smaller tail probability → safer projection.

🔢 Intuition: Why log N?

The union bound argument, step by step

The union bound argument

We want: P(any of the $\binom{N}{2}$ pairs has distortion $>\varepsilon$) to be small.

Step 1. For one pair $(u,v)$, let $D_{uv} = \|f(u)-f(v)\|^2 / \|u-v\|^2$. We showed:

$$P(|D_{uv} - 1| > \varepsilon) \;\leq\; 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$$

Step 2. Union bound over all $\binom{N}{2} < N^2/2$ pairs:

$$P\!\left(\exists\, u,v:\; |D_{uv} - 1| > \varepsilon\right) \;\leq\; \frac{N^2}{2} \cdot 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8} = N^2 e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$$

Step 3. We want this $< \delta$ (say $\delta = 0.01$). Solve for $k$:

$$N^2 e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8} < \delta$$ $$e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8} < \frac{\delta}{N^2}$$ $$-\frac{k\varepsilon^2}{8} < \ln\frac{\delta}{N^2} = \ln\delta - 2\ln N$$ $$k > \frac{8(2\ln N + \ln(1/\delta))}{\varepsilon^2}$$

Step 4. For $\delta = 1$ (any positive probability): $k > \frac{16\ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$

Absorbing constants: $k = O\!\left(\frac{\log N}{\varepsilon^2}\right)$   ← the JL bound.

Why is it $\log N$ and not $N$?
The union bound costs $N^2$, but the exponential decay $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ beats $N^2$ with only $k = O(\log N)$ — because $e^{k} \gg N^2$ for $k = c\log N$, any $c > 0$.

Deep dive: Nearest-Neighbor Search

Given a database of $N$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$ and a query $q$, find the vector closest to $q$. Naively: $O(Nd)$ per query.

With JL:

  1. Project all $N$ vectors and $q$ to $\mathbb{R}^k$ (one-time cost: $O(Nd)$)
  2. Build an index on the $k$-dimensional projected vectors
  3. At query time: project $q$ ($O(k)$), search the $k$-dim index ($O(\log N)$)

The JL guarantee ensures that the nearest neighbor in $\mathbb{R}^k$ is a $(1+\varepsilon)$-approximate nearest neighbor in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

This was formalized by Indyk & Motwani (1998) as Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH) — one of the most influential algorithms in the era of large-scale data, now used in every major vector database.

Deep dive: Least-Squares Sketching

Solve $\min_{\mathbf{x}} \|A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}\|^2$ where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ with $N \gg d$. Cost: $O(Nd^2)$.

With JL (the sketch-and-solve approach):

  1. Draw a JL matrix $S \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times N}$ with $k = O(d/\varepsilon^2)$
  2. Compress: $\tilde{A} = SA \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$, $\tilde{b} = S\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^k$
  3. Solve the small system: $\min_{\mathbf{x}} \|\tilde{A}\mathbf{x} - \tilde{b}\|^2$ in $O(kd^2)$

The result $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ satisfies $\|A\hat{\mathbf{x}} - \mathbf{b}\|^2 \leq (1+\varepsilon)\|A\mathbf{x}^* - \mathbf{b}\|^2$ — a $(1+\varepsilon)$-approximate solution.

Cost: $O(Nd \cdot k/N + kd^2) = O(kd + kd^2) = O(kd^2)$ — independent of $N$! Once $N$ is large, sketching dominates.

What is the KV Cache?

In transformer attention, each token produces a Key (K) and Value (V) vector. To generate token $t+1$, the model must attend over all previous tokens 1 through $t$, requiring access to all their K and V vectors:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right) V$$

The KV cache stores these K and V vectors so they don't need to be recomputed. But it grows linearly with context length.

Stage 1 — PolarQuant

Convert each key/value vector from Cartesian to polar coordinates, separating it into a magnitude (radius) and a set of angles:

$$\mathbf{x} = [x_1, \ldots, x_d] \;\longrightarrow\; (r, \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_{d-1})$$

Why polar? The angular components of random Gaussian vectors follow predictable, concentrated distributions. This lets PolarQuant apply a standard scalar quantizer to the angles without per-block normalization — eliminating a major source of overhead.

PolarQuant captures ~95% of the information in the original vector. The remaining residual error is small but non-zero — and it introduces inner-product bias.

PolarQuant alone is presented at AISTATS 2026.

Stage 2 — QJL (Quantized JL Transform)

The residual from Stage 1 is:

$$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{x} - \text{PolarQuant}^{-1}(\text{PolarQuant}(\mathbf{x}))$$

Apply a JL random projection to $\mathbf{r}$, then quantize to a single sign bit per dimension:

$$\mathbf{q} = \text{sign}(A\mathbf{r}) \in \{+1,-1\}^k$$

Why does 1 bit suffice? The JL guarantee ensures that inner products involving $\mathbf{r}$ are preserved by even 1-bit projections — because the sign of the JL-projected residual carries enough information to correct the attention bias.

QJL was independently published at AAAI 2025. TurboQuant combines it with PolarQuant for the first time.

The combined output uses most bits on the coarse polar representation and wastes zero bits on normalization constants.

How JL enables zero-bias quantization

The core operation in attention is the inner product $\langle Q_t, K_s \rangle$ between the current query $Q_t$ and each cached key $K_s$. Getting this inner product right is what matters for attention accuracy.

Standard quantization (int4, int8) approximates each $K_s$ with $\hat{K}_s$, introducing a systematic error:

$$\langle Q_t, K_s \rangle \neq \langle Q_t, \hat{K}_s \rangle$$

The error $\langle Q_t, K_s - \hat{K}_s \rangle$ is non-zero on average — this is the inner-product bias problem.

The JL fix

TurboQuant's QJL stage corrects this bias. The residual $\mathbf{r} = K_s - \hat{K}_s$ is encoded via a random JL matrix $A$:

$$\hat{\mathbf{r}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} A^\top \text{sign}(A\mathbf{r})$$

By the JL guarantee, for any query $Q_t$:

$$\langle Q_t, \hat{\mathbf{r}} \rangle \approx \langle Q_t, \mathbf{r} \rangle$$
The JL lemma guarantees this approximation holds for all queries $Q_t$ simultaneously — not just in expectation. This is the key property that enables zero accuracy loss at inference.

🧮 Proof Toolkit: Key Inequalities

Three results from probability theory — developed from scratch — that power the JL proof.

The chain: Markov → (applied to $(X-\mu)^2$) → Chebyshev → (applied to $e^{tX}$) → Chernoff → (applied to each pair, then) → Union Bound → JL Lemma.

Markov's Inequality

Statement. For any non-negative random variable $X$ and any $a > 0$: $$P(X \geq a) \;\leq\; \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a}$$

Proof (3 lines)

Write out the expectation, split the integral at $a$, then lower-bound the integrand on $[a, \infty)$ by $a$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty x\,f(x)\,dx \;\geq\; \int_a^\infty x\,f(x)\,dx \;\geq\; \int_a^\infty a\,f(x)\,dx = a\cdot P(X \geq a)$$

Divide both sides by $a$.  ∎

Why the first inequality holds

We dropped the integral over $[0, a)$. Since $X \geq 0$, the integrand $x\,f(x) \geq 0$ everywhere, so removing a region can only decrease the integral:

$$\int_0^\infty x\,f(x)\,dx \;\geq\; \int_a^\infty x\,f(x)\,dx$$
Why the second inequality holds

On the region $[a, \infty)$, we have $x \geq a$, so $x\,f(x) \geq a\,f(x)$ pointwise:

$$\int_a^\infty x\,f(x)\,dx \;\geq\; \int_a^\infty a\,f(x)\,dx = a \int_a^\infty f(x)\,dx = a\cdot P(X \geq a)$$

Interpretation

If the average is $\mu$, then the probability of exceeding $a$ can be no larger than $\mu/a$. Intuitively: the "weight" above $a$ can't pull the mean above $\mu$.

Limitation — polynomial decay. The bound $\mu/a$ decays as $1/a$. For $a = 10\mu$, Markov only says $P(X \geq 10\mu) \leq 0.1$ — regardless of how concentrated $X$ is. A tight exponential distribution and a heavy-tailed one get the same bound.

What Markov doesn't use

The proof uses only $X \geq 0$ and linearity of expectation. It knows nothing about the shape, variance, or higher moments of $X$. This is why it's so general — and why it can be so loose.

Chebyshev's Inequality

Chebyshev is Markov applied to a cleverly chosen non-negative random variable.

Derivation from Markov

Let $X$ be any random variable with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Define $Y = (X - \mu)^2$. Then:

  • $Y \geq 0$ ✓ (it's a square)
  • $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2] = \sigma^2$

Apply Markov to $Y$ with threshold $t^2$:

$$P\!\left(Y \geq t^2\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y]}{t^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2}$$

But $Y \geq t^2$ is exactly $|X - \mu| \geq t$, so:

$$P\!\left(|X - \mu| \geq t\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2} \quad \text{(Chebyshev)}$$

In terms of standard deviations ($t = k\sigma$): $P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq 1/k^2$.

What Chebyshev uses that Markov doesn't

Chebyshev uses the variance $\sigma^2$. Markov uses only the mean. As a result, Chebyshev gives a $1/t^2$ decay instead of $1/t$.

Example: Law of Large Numbers

Let $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ with each $X_i$ i.i.d., mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$. Then $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \sigma^2/n$, so:

$$P\!\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \;\to\; 0 \text{ as } n \to \infty$$

This is a proof of the Weak Law of Large Numbers using only Chebyshev.

Chebyshev is distribution-free — it applies to any distribution with finite variance. This generality comes at a cost: it can't exploit the specific shape of the distribution. For the JL proof, we know the distribution exactly ($\chi^2(k)$), so we can do much better with Chernoff.

Why Chebyshev isn't enough for JL

For the JL union bound to work, we need the per-pair failure probability to be much smaller than $1/N^2$. Chebyshev gives:

$$P\!\left(\left|\frac{\chi^2(k)}{k} - 1\right| > \varepsilon\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\text{Var}(\chi^2(k)/k)}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{2/k}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{2}{k\varepsilon^2}$$

Setting this below $1/N^2$ requires $k \geq 2N^2/\varepsilon^2$ — a polynomial in $N$, not $\log N$. The Chernoff bound is essential.

Moment Generating Functions (MGFs)

Definition. The MGF of a random variable $X$ is: $$M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{tX}\right]$$ defined for all $t$ where the expectation is finite.

Why "moment generating"?

Expanding $e^{tX} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(tX)^k}{k!}$ and taking expectations:

$$M_X(t) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}\,\mathbb{E}[X^k]$$

Therefore $M_X^{(k)}(0) = \mathbb{E}[X^k]$ — differentiating $k$ times at $t=0$ recovers the $k$-th moment. The MGF encodes all moments of the distribution.

Deriving the $\chi^2(k)$ MGF

If $X \sim \chi^2(k)$ then $X = Z_1^2 + \cdots + Z_k^2$ with $Z_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. By independence:

$$M_X(t) = \prod_{i=1}^k M_{Z_i^2}(t) = \left[M_{Z^2}(t)\right]^k$$

For $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:

$$M_{Z^2}(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tZ^2}] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz^2} \cdot \frac{e^{-z^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,dz = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{-z^2(1-2t)/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,dz$$

This integral is a Gaussian integral: $\int e^{-\alpha z^2/2}dz = \sqrt{2\pi/\alpha}$ with $\alpha = 1-2t$ (valid for $t < 1/2$):

$$M_{Z^2}(t) = \frac{\sqrt{2\pi/(1-2t)}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} = (1-2t)^{-1/2}$$

Therefore:

$$M_{\chi^2(k)}(t) = \left[(1-2t)^{-1/2}\right]^k = (1-2t)^{-k/2}, \quad t < \tfrac{1}{2}$$

This is the MGF we plug into the Chernoff bound in Step 3 of the JL proof.

Key intuition: the MGF encodes exponential tail information. If $M_X(t)$ grows slowly as a function of $t$, then $X$ has light tails. The Chernoff bound exploits this by optimizing the ratio $M_X(t)/e^{ta}$ over $t$.

The Chernoff Bound

Core idea: apply Markov's inequality not to $X$ itself, but to the non-negative random variable $e^{tX}$.

Derivation

For any $t > 0$ and any $a$:

$$P(X \geq a) = P\!\left(e^{tX} \geq e^{ta}\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]}{e^{ta}} = \frac{M_X(t)}{e^{ta}}$$

The first step is valid because $x \mapsto e^{tx}$ is strictly increasing (for $t>0$). The inequality is Markov applied to $e^{tX}$. Since this holds for every $t > 0$, take the infimum:

$$P(X \geq a) \;\leq\; \inf_{t > 0} \frac{M_X(t)}{e^{ta}} = \inf_{t > 0} e^{\log M_X(t) - ta}$$

The optimization $\inf_t [\log M_X(t) - ta]$ is the Legendre–Fenchel transform of the log-MGF — a convex function minimized by setting $M_X'(t)/M_X(t) = a$, i.e., $\mathbb{E}[Xe^{tX}]/\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = a$.

Why exponential improvement over Markov?

Markov gives $P(X \geq a) \leq \mu/a$ — decays as $1/a$. Chernoff gives $e^{-I(a)}$ where $I(a)$ is the rate function. For Gaussians and chi-squared distributions, $I(a)$ is quadratic in $a$, giving decay $\sim e^{-ca^2}$ — exponentially faster.

Applied to $\chi^2(k)/k$ (the JL case)

Let $Y = \chi^2(k)/k$. We need both tails.

Upper tail: $P(Y \geq 1+\varepsilon)$

$P(kY \geq k(1+\varepsilon)) \leq \inf_{0 < t < 1/2} (1-2t)^{-k/2} e^{-tk(1+\varepsilon)}$.

Take log and differentiate to find optimal $t^* = \frac{\varepsilon}{2(1+\varepsilon)}$. Substituting and simplifying using $\ln(1+\varepsilon) \geq \varepsilon - \varepsilon^2/2$:

$$P(Y \geq 1+\varepsilon) \;\leq\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{k}{2}\left[(1+\varepsilon)\ln(1+\varepsilon) - \varepsilon\right]\right) \;\leq\; e^{-k\varepsilon^2/4}$$

Lower tail: $P(Y \leq 1-\varepsilon)$

Similar argument with $t < 0$. Optimal $t^* = -\frac{\varepsilon}{2(1-\varepsilon)}$ gives:

$$P(Y \leq 1-\varepsilon) \;\leq\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{k}{2}\left[\varepsilon + \ln(1-\varepsilon)\right]\right) \;\leq\; e^{-k\varepsilon^2/4}$$

Two-sided, simplified:

$$P(|Y - 1| > \varepsilon) \;\leq\; 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/4} \;\leq\; 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$$ (using $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/4} \leq e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ for any $k, \varepsilon > 0$; the $/8$ form is looser but conventional)

The key trick in one line

Markov on $X$:   $P(X \geq a) \leq \mu / a$

Markov on $(X-\mu)^2$:   $P(|X-\mu| \geq t) \leq \sigma^2/t^2$

Markov on $e^{tX}$:   $P(X \geq a) \leq M_X(t) \cdot e^{-ta}$

Each step plugs a more informative non-negative function into Markov. $e^{tX}$ is the best because it is sensitive to the entire distribution, not just one moment.

The Union Bound (Boole's Inequality)

Statement. For any events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_m$ (no independence required): $$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^m A_i\right) \;\leq\; \sum_{i=1}^m P(A_i)$$

Proof

Define disjoint events $B_1 = A_1$ and $B_i = A_i \setminus (A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_{i-1})$ for $i \geq 2$. Then $\cup B_i = \cup A_i$ and $B_i \subseteq A_i$, so:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i\right) = P\!\left(\bigsqcup_i B_i\right) = \sum_i P(B_i) \;\leq\; \sum_i P(A_i) \quad \square$$

Alternatively, by inclusion-exclusion: $P(\cup A_i) = \sum P(A_i) - \sum_{i

Why "no independence required" matters

In the JL proof, the events $B_{uv}$ = "pair $(u,v)$ is badly projected" are not independent — if two pairs share a point (e.g., $(u,v)$ and $(u,w)$), their distortions are correlated. The union bound works regardless.

When is the union bound tight?

The union bound is exactly tight when the events are disjoint: $P(\cup A_i) = \sum P(A_i)$. It can be very loose when events overlap heavily — in the JL case, many pairs share endpoints, so the true failure probability is much lower than the union bound suggests.

Quantitative form for JL

In JL, we have $m = \binom{N}{2} < N^2/2$ events, each with probability $\leq 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$:

$$P(\text{any pair fails}) \leq \frac{N^2}{2} \cdot 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8} = N^2 e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$$

Setting this $< 1$ and solving for $k$:

$$N^2 e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8} < 1 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; k > \frac{8 \cdot 2\ln N}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{16 \ln N}{\varepsilon^2}$$

The deep reason $k = O(\log N)$:

The exponential function $e^{k\varepsilon^2/8}$ grows as $N^{c}$ when $k = \frac{8c}{\varepsilon^2}\ln N$. So with just $k = O(\log N/\varepsilon^2)$ projection dimensions, the Chernoff tail probability $e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8} = N^{-c}$ shrinks faster than $1/N^2$, making the union bound over $N^2$ pairs go to zero. The logarithm in $k$ is simply the inverse of the exponent in the Chernoff tail — they cancel perfectly.
Analogy with the birthday problem: if $m$ events each have probability $p$, you need $m \cdot p < 1$ for the union bound to guarantee a good outcome. Since $p = 2e^{-k\varepsilon^2/8}$ is exponentially small in $k$, and $m = N^2$ is polynomially large in $N$, you only need $k \sim \log N$ to bring $m \cdot p$ below 1 — that's the heart of the JL argument.

Gaussian Random Projection

Authors: Johnson & Lindenstrauss (1984); clean proof by Dasgupta & Gupta (2001).

Construction

Draw a matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ with each entry $A_{ij} \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The projection is:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}\,A\mathbf{x} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^k$$

Why it works

Each projected coordinate $(A\mathbf{x})_i = \mathbf{a}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}$ is a sum of $d$ independent Gaussians, hence $\mathcal{N}(0, \|\mathbf{x}\|^2)$. The squared norm $\|A\mathbf{x}\|^2/k$ therefore follows a $\chi^2(k)/k$ distribution concentrated around 1 — this is the foundation of the entire proof (see Section 2 and Proof Toolkit).

Rotational invariance

A key property of the Gaussian distribution: it is rotationally symmetric. This means the projection $A\mathbf{x}$ has the same distribution for all unit vectors $\mathbf{x}$, regardless of direction. This makes the proof clean and the guarantee direction-independent.

Complexity

For $d = 100{,}000$ and $k = 1{,}000$: storing $A$ requires 800 MB. Applying it requires 100M multiply-adds per vector. This motivates all subsequent transforms.

JL constant

The Gaussian transform achieves the optimal JL constant: among all linear maps, the Gaussian random projection minimizes the required $k$ for a given $(\varepsilon, \delta)$ guarantee. This optimality (shown by various lower bounds) means no linear JL transform can do better in the worst case — only in special cases (structured inputs) can other transforms improve.

The distribution of a single projected coordinate

For fixed $\mathbf{x}$ with $\|\mathbf{x}\|=1$, the $i$-th output coordinate is:

$$\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}A\mathbf{x}\right)_i = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} \sum_{j=1}^d A_{ij} x_j \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \frac{1}{k}\right)$$

So the expected squared projection is $\mathbb{E}[(f(\mathbf{x}))_i^2] = 1/k$, and summing $k$ such coordinates:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\|f(\mathbf{x})\|^2\right] = k \cdot \frac{1}{k} = 1 = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 \;\checkmark$$

Generating efficiently

In practice, $A$ can be generated on the fly using a pseudorandom number generator seeded with a shared key — both encoder and decoder can reconstruct $A$ without transmitting it. This reduces storage from $O(dk)$ floats to $O(1)$ (just the seed).

Practical note: most software libraries (NumPy, PyTorch, MATLAB) implement highly optimized BLAS matrix-vector products. For moderate $d$ and $k$, the Gaussian transform is often the fastest in wall-clock time despite its $O(dk)$ complexity, because BLAS routines exploit CPU vectorization and cache efficiently.
When to use: small $d$ (≤ 10,000), when simplicity matters, when you need the tightest theoretical guarantee, or when using GPU acceleration (matrix multiply is heavily optimized on GPUs).

Achlioptas / Database-Friendly Transform

Author: Dimitris Achlioptas, 2003. "Database-friendly random projections: Johnson-Lindenstrauss with binary coins."

Motivation

The Gaussian transform requires generating and storing floating-point random numbers — expensive in both memory and compute. Achlioptas asked: can we replace the Gaussian entries with simple discrete values and still get a JL guarantee?

Construction — two variants

Variant 1 (±1): Each entry $A_{ij}$ is drawn i.i.d. as:

$$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} +1 & \text{with probability } \tfrac{1}{2} \\ -1 & \text{with probability } \tfrac{1}{2} \end{cases}$$

Apply as $f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} A\mathbf{x}$.

Variant 2 (sparse ±√3): Each entry is:

$$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} +\!\sqrt{3} & \text{with probability } \tfrac{1}{6} \\ 0 & \text{with probability } \tfrac{2}{3} \\ -\!\sqrt{3} & \text{with probability } \tfrac{1}{6} \end{cases}$$

Why it still works

The proof only needs two conditions on the distribution of entries:

  1. $\mathbb{E}[A_{ij}] = 0$ (zero mean → projection is unbiased)
  2. $\mathbb{E}[A_{ij}^2] = 1$ (unit variance → scaling works out)

Both the ±1 distribution ($\mathbb{E}[A^2] = 1$) and the sparse ±√3 distribution ($\mathbb{E}[A^2] = \tfrac{1}{6}\cdot 3 + \tfrac{1}{6}\cdot 3 = 1$) satisfy these conditions. The concentration proof uses higher moments, which are also well-controlled for these distributions.

Key insight for the sparse variant: 2/3 of multiplications become multiplications by 0, which can be skipped entirely. This gives a $3\times$ speedup in the apply step over the dense variants — for free, just by sparsifying the random matrix.

JL guarantee

Both variants satisfy the JL lemma with the same asymptotic bound $k = O(\log N / \varepsilon^2)$. The constant factor may be slightly worse than Gaussian, but in practice the difference is negligible.

Practical advantages

  • Memory: 1 bit vs 32 bits per entry → $32\times$ memory reduction
  • Speed: integer/bitwise operations are faster than floating-point multiply on most hardware
  • Cache: bit-packed matrices fit in cache far better
  • Reproducibility: deterministic from a seed, easy to regenerate
When to use: large $d$ and $k$ where memory is a bottleneck, or when floating-point arithmetic is slow (e.g., embedded systems). The sparse variant is particularly effective for dense input vectors.

Fast JL Transform (FJLT)

Authors: Nir Ailon & Bernard Chazelle, 2006. "The Fast Johnson-Lindenstrauss Transform and Approximate Nearest Neighbors." SICOMP 2009.

Motivation

Both Gaussian and Achlioptas require $O(dk)$ work per vector. But $k = O(\log N / \varepsilon^2)$, so this is $O(d \log N / \varepsilon^2)$. For $N \gg 1$ (large datasets), can we do better?

FJLT achieves $O(d \log d)$ — independent of $k$ — by replacing the explicit matrix with a structured transform.

Construction

The FJLT projection is the composition of three maps:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}\, P\, H\, D\, \mathbf{x}$$

applied right-to-left:

  1. $D \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$: diagonal matrix with i.i.d. random signs $D_{ii} \sim \text{Uniform}\{+1,-1\}$. Randomizes the phases of $\mathbf{x}$.
  2. $H \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$: the Walsh-Hadamard Transform (WHT) matrix, defined recursively: $H_1 = [1]$, $H_{2^l} = \begin{bmatrix}H_{2^{l-1}} & H_{2^{l-1}} \\ H_{2^{l-1}} & -H_{2^{l-1}}\end{bmatrix}$. Computed in $O(d \log d)$ via the Fast Hadamard Transform (FHT). No matrix is stored — it's applied as a butterfly algorithm.
  3. $P \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$: a sparse Gaussian matrix (or uniform subsampling). Selects and rescales $k$ coordinates. Applied in $O(dk)$ but $k \ll d$, or $O(d)$ for pure subsampling.

Total cost

$D\mathbf{x}$: $O(d)$ sign flips. $HD\mathbf{x}$: $O(d\log d)$ Fast Hadamard. $PHD\mathbf{x}$: $O(dk_{\text{eff}})$ or $O(k)$. Total: $O(d\log d)$, independent of $k$.

Why $D$ is needed

Without $D$, the Hadamard transform is deterministic. A sparse input (many zeros) would concentrate its energy in a few Hadamard coefficients, and uniform subsampling would miss most of the information. The random sign matrix $D$ spreads the energy uniformly across all Hadamard coefficients before subsampling — this is the key insight.

SRHT: Subsampled Randomized Hadamard Transform

A related construction (Tropp, 2011; Ailon & Liberty, 2011):

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{d}{k}}\, S\, H\, D\, \mathbf{x}$$

where $S \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ is a uniform random row-sampling matrix (each of the $d$ coordinates is independently included with probability $k/d$). Apply cost: $O(d\log d)$.

The Hadamard matrix: $H_d$ is a $d\times d$ matrix with $\pm 1$ entries satisfying $H_d H_d^\top = d\,I$. It is the real analogue of the DFT. Each stage is $O(d)$ additions. With $\log_2 d$ stages total: $O(d\log d)$. No multiplication anywhere.

Sparse JL Transform

Authors: Daniel Kane & Jelani Nelson, 2014. "Sparser Johnson-Lindenstrauss Transforms."

Setting: sparse input vectors

Many real-world vectors are sparse: TF-IDF text features, one-hot encodings, genomic data, graph adjacency rows. If $\mathbf{x}$ has only $s$ non-zero entries, then applying a dense $k \times d$ matrix still costs $O(dk)$, ignoring the sparsity of $\mathbf{x}$. Can we build a sparse $A$ that exploits sparse inputs?

Construction

Each column $j$ of $A$ has exactly $q$ non-zero entries, placed uniformly at random among the $k$ rows, each with value $\pm 1/\sqrt{q}$ (random sign):

$$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} \pm 1/\sqrt{q} & \text{if row } i \text{ selected for column } j \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

For column $j$: independently choose $q$ of the $k$ rows without replacement, assign random ±1 signs. Repeat for each of the $d$ columns.

Apply cost for sparse inputs

For a vector $\mathbf{x}$ with $s$ non-zeros:

$$f(\mathbf{x})_i = \sum_{j:\,x_j\neq 0} A_{ij}\,x_j$$

Each non-zero $x_j$ touches at most $q$ rows of $A$ (the non-zeros in column $j$). Total work: $O(qs)$, compared to $O(dk)$ for dense.

Required sparsity $q$

Kane & Nelson showed that setting $q = O(\varepsilon^{-1} \log(1/\delta))$ suffices for the JL guarantee (distortion $\leq \varepsilon$ with probability $\geq 1-\delta$). Crucially, $q$ is independent of $d$ and depends only weakly on $k$.

Count Sketch (extreme sparsity)

The limiting case of Sparse JL sets $q = 1$ (one non-zero per column):

$$f(\mathbf{x})_i = \sum_{j:\,h(j)=i} \sigma_j\, x_j$$

where $h: [d] \to [k]$ is a random hash and $\sigma_j \sim \{+1,-1\}$ are random signs.

Apply cost: $O(d)$ — each coordinate $x_j$ contributes to exactly one bucket.

Limitation: with $q=1$ the concentration is weaker. Count Sketch preserves $\ell_2$ norms approximately only when $k = \Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$, with a worse constant than Sparse JL. It is primarily used for frequency estimation and streaming, not as a general-purpose JL transform.

Intuition for why sparsity works: The JL proof only needs each column to have zero mean and unit variance. With $q$ random ±$1/\sqrt{q}$ entries per column, the expected squared norm is $q \cdot (1/q) = 1$ ✓, and the variance is $O(1/q)$. As long as $q$ is large enough relative to $\varepsilon$, concentration holds. The key insight is that $q$ does not need to scale with $d$ — the $d$ dimensions of the input can be handled one column at a time.
When to use: text/NLP pipelines with TF-IDF or bag-of-words vectors (very sparse), genomic data, graph Laplacian problems. If your input is dense, Sparse JL offers less benefit — FJLT or Gaussian are better choices.

Lower bound: you can't go below $q = \Omega(\varepsilon^{-1})$

Nelson & Nguyen (2013) proved that any JL transform (linear or nonlinear) achieving distortion $\varepsilon$ requires at least $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ bits per projected dimension. Since each dimension contributes $O(\log(1/\varepsilon))$ bits, the sparsity $q$ of any column of a linear JL map must satisfy $q = \Omega(\varepsilon^{-1}/\log(1/\varepsilon))$. Kane & Nelson's construction is nearly optimal.

Quantized JL (QJL)

Authors: Zandieh, Han, Mirrokni, Karbasi, 2024. "QJL: 1-Bit Quantized JL Transform for KV Cache Quantization with Zero Overhead." AAAI 2025.

Motivation: beyond distance preservation

All previous transforms preserve Euclidean distances by mapping $\mathbf{x} \mapsto f(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^k$. QJL asks: what if we only need to preserve inner products, and we store each projected coordinate as a single bit?

Construction

Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ be a Gaussian random matrix. The QJL map is:

$$f_{\text{QJL}}(\mathbf{x}) = \text{sign}(A\mathbf{x}) \in \{+1,-1\}^k$$

Store only the $k$ sign bits — one bit per projected dimension.

What is preserved?

Unlike standard JL which preserves distances, QJL preserves angular similarity via the connection between random projections and angular distance:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\text{sign}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{x})\cdot\text{sign}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{y})\right] = 1 - \frac{2}{\pi}\,\theta_{xy}$$

where $\theta_{xy}$ is the angle between $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$, and $\mathbf{a}$ is a random Gaussian vector. This is related to SimHash / locality-sensitive hashing for cosine similarity.

Inner product estimation

For inner products, the QJL estimator is:

$$\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle \;\approx\; \|\mathbf{x}\|\,\|\mathbf{y}\|\,\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \cdot \frac{d_H(f(\mathbf{x}),\, f(\mathbf{y}))}{k/2}\right)$$

where $d_H$ is the Hamming distance between the two $k$-bit strings. This can be computed extremely fast using popcount CPU instructions ($O(k/64)$ words).

Error analysis

For unit vectors $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$ with $k$ QJL bits:

$$\left|\hat{\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\rangle} - \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\rangle\right| \;\leq\; O\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{k}}\right)$$

with high probability. This is the same $O(1/\sqrt{k})$ rate as unquantized JL — quantization adds only a small constant factor, not an additional error term.

Role in TurboQuant

TurboQuant uses QJL as its residual correction stage (Stage 2). After PolarQuant captures the bulk of each KV vector, the residual $\mathbf{r}$ is stored as $\text{sign}(A\mathbf{r})$. The JL guarantee ensures:

$$\langle Q_t,\, A^\top \text{sign}(A\mathbf{r})/\sqrt{k}\rangle \;\approx\; \langle Q_t, \mathbf{r}\rangle$$

for all query vectors $Q_t$ simultaneously, correcting the inner-product bias from PolarQuant. This is what makes TurboQuant achieve zero accuracy loss: the JL guarantee holds for all queries without any per-query calibration.

QJL is fundamentally different from 1-bit JL: it does not try to preserve Euclidean distances with 1 bit — that is provably impossible. Instead it preserves inner products (which is what attention needs), leveraging the angular geometry of the Gaussian random projection.
When to use: any application that needs inner products or cosine similarities at extreme memory efficiency — LLM KV caches, maximum inner product search (MIPS), dense retrieval, approximate similarity join.
