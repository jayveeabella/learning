Lie Groups

Continuous symmetries, starting from the humble circle — Sophus Lie, 1873

A Lie group (pronounced "Lee") is simultaneously a group — you can compose symmetries — and a smooth manifold — you can do calculus on it. The combination is astonishingly powerful: it means continuous symmetries have both algebraic structure and differential geometry at once.

The simplest non-trivial Lie group is the circle $S^1$. A point on the unit circle represents a rotation of the plane by some angle $\theta$. Two rotations compose by adding their angles. That's the whole group law. But because the circle is also a smooth 1-dimensional manifold, you can ask: what does a rotation look like when it is infinitesimally close to "no rotation at all"? Answering that question gives you the Lie algebra and the exponential map.

The central picture: Every Lie group $G$ has a Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ — a flat, linear space living at the identity element, like a tangent plane to a curved surface. The exponential map $\exp: \mathfrak{g} \to G$ translates between this flat world and the curved world of the group. For the circle, $\exp(i\theta) = e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ — Euler's formula is Lie theory.

Mastering the circle gives you a template that scales directly to the 3D rotation group $SO(3)$ used in robotics and graphics, to $SU(2)$ describing quantum spin, and to the gauge groups underlying all of particle physics.

⭕ The Circle Group

Before generalizing, let's spend quality time with the one example that makes everything concrete. The circle group appears in three equivalent guises:

$U(1)$ is the group of $1\times1$ complex unitary matrices — equivalently, complex numbers of magnitude 1:

$$U(1) = \{ z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| = 1 \} = \{ e^{i\theta} : \theta \in \mathbb{R} \}$$

The group operation is complex multiplication. Since $|e^{i\alpha}| = |e^{i\beta}| = 1$, their product also has magnitude 1:

$$e^{i\alpha} \cdot e^{i\beta} = e^{i(\alpha+\beta)}$$

Multiplication of unit complex numbers is exactly angle addition.

The identity element is $1 = e^{i \cdot 0}$ (no rotation). The inverse of $e^{i\theta}$ is $e^{-i\theta} = \overline{e^{i\theta}}$ (the complex conjugate — a rotation backwards by $\theta$).
Key fact: $U(1)$ is abelian — the group is commutative: $e^{i\alpha} e^{i\beta} = e^{i\beta} e^{i\alpha}$. Rotating by $\alpha$ then $\beta$ gives the same result as $\beta$ then $\alpha$. Most higher Lie groups (like $SO(3)$) are non-abelian.

$SO(2)$ ("Special Orthogonal group in 2 dimensions") is the group of $2\times2$ rotation matrices:

$$SO(2) = \left\{ R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} : \theta \in \mathbb{R} \right\}$$

"Special" means $\det R_\theta = 1$ (not a reflection). "Orthogonal" means $R_\theta^T R_\theta = I$. The group operation is matrix multiplication, and one can verify directly:

$$R_\alpha R_\beta = R_{\alpha+\beta}$$

This is identical to the $U(1)$ law! The map $e^{i\theta} \mapsto R_\theta$ is a Lie group isomorphism: $U(1) \cong SO(2)$. They are the same abstract group wearing different coordinate systems.

The Lie algebra description changes between the two: for $U(1)$ the Lie algebra is $i\mathbb{R}$ (imaginary numbers), while for $SO(2)$ the Lie algebra is $2\times2$ antisymmetric matrices $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-v\\v&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$. The isomorphism sends $iv \mapsto \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-v\\v&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$.

The most elementary description: the circle group is just real numbers modulo $2\pi$:

$$\mathbb{R} / 2\pi\mathbb{Z} = \{ \theta \in \mathbb{R} \} \big/ (\theta \sim \theta + 2\pi)$$

The group operation is addition mod $2\pi$. This makes the structure transparent:

  • It's obviously 1-dimensional (a real parameter $\theta$)
  • It's compact — $\theta$ lives in a bounded interval $[0, 2\pi)$
  • It wraps around — $0$ and $2\pi$ are the same point
The circle is not simply connected: a loop that goes all the way around cannot be contracted to a point. Its fundamental group is $\pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$ (the winding number). This topological fact underlies why the exponential map $\mathbb{R} \to S^1$ is many-to-one.

Why $S^1$ is the perfect starting point

Every 1-dimensional connected Lie group is isomorphic to either $\mathbb{R}$ (the real line under addition, non-compact) or $S^1$ (the circle, compact). The circle is the simplest compact Lie group. Compact Lie groups have beautiful properties: every element is in the image of the exponential map, the group has finite volume, and its representation theory (generalised Fourier analysis) is especially clean. Moreover:

  • Every compact abelian Lie group is a product of circles (a torus $T^n = S^1 \times \cdots \times S^1$)
  • The characters (1D representations) of $U(1)$ are precisely $e^{in\theta}$, $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ — the Fourier basis
  • $U(1)$ is the gauge group of electromagnetism; understanding it is prerequisite to all gauge theory

📐 The Lie Algebra

A Lie group is a curved space — the circle bends back on itself. But at any single point, it looks flat: zoom in far enough and any curve looks like a straight line. The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ captures this flat, linear picture at the special point — the identity — and packages it as a vector space.

Tangent space at the identity

Take any smooth curve $\gamma: \mathbb{R} \to G$ passing through the identity at $t = 0$: $\gamma(0) = e$. Its derivative $\gamma'(0)$ is a tangent vector at $e$. Collecting all such derivatives gives the Lie algebra:

$$\mathfrak{g} = T_e G = \bigl\{ \gamma'(0) \,:\, \gamma \text{ smooth}, \gamma(0) = e \bigr\}$$

The Lie algebra of $U(1)$: why imaginary?

For a curve $\gamma(t) \in U(1)$, the constraint $|\gamma(t)|^2 = 1$ must hold for all $t$. Differentiate both sides at $t=0$ (using $|\gamma|^2 = \overline{\gamma}\,\gamma$):

$$\frac{d}{dt}\bigl[\overline{\gamma(t)}\,\gamma(t)\bigr]_{t=0} = 0 \implies \overline{\gamma'(0)}\cdot 1 + 1 \cdot \gamma'(0) = 0 \implies \gamma'(0) + \overline{\gamma'(0)} = 0 \implies 2\,\text{Re}(\gamma'(0)) = 0$$

The tangent vector must be purely imaginary. Therefore:

$$\mathfrak{u}(1) = T_1\,U(1) = i\mathbb{R} = \{iv : v \in \mathbb{R}\}$$
The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{u}(1) \cong \mathbb{R}$ is 1-dimensional — just a line. This makes sense: the circle is a 1-dimensional manifold, so its tangent space at any point is 1-dimensional too. Concretely, the tangent line at $1 = (1,0) \in S^1$ points straight up and down — in the purely imaginary direction.

Geometric picture

Draw the unit circle. The identity is at $(1, 0)$. The tangent line there runs vertically — in the direction of the imaginary axis. Every tangent vector at the identity is a multiple of $i$: it is a purely imaginary number $iv$.

A small element $iv \in \mathfrak{u}(1)$ represents an infinitesimal rotation by angle $v$. The exponential map turns this infinitesimal rotation into a finite one: $\exp(iv) = e^{iv}$ is the point on the circle at angle $v$.

For small $v$: $e^{iv} \approx 1 + iv$ (first-order Taylor expansion), so the point on the circle is approximately the point on the tangent line. This is the sense in which the Lie algebra is a linear approximation to the group.

The Lie bracket

The Lie algebra has an additional operation called the Lie bracket $[X, Y]$, which encodes how "infinitesimal symmetries fail to commute." For any Lie group, the bracket of two tangent vectors $X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}$ measures the infinitesimal non-commutativity:

$$[X, Y] = XY - YX \quad \text{(for matrix groups)}$$

For $U(1)$, since the group is abelian ($e^{i\alpha}e^{i\beta} = e^{i\beta}e^{i\alpha}$), the bracket is identically zero:

$$[iv_1, iv_2] = (iv_1)(iv_2) - (iv_2)(iv_1) = 0$$

A zero bracket is the algebraic signature of commutativity. For $SO(3)$, the bracket $[L_x, L_y] = L_z$ gives the cross product — rotations around different axes don't commute.

Lie algebra of $SO(2)$: antisymmetric matrices

For $SO(2)$, a curve through the identity satisfies $R(t)^T R(t) = I$. Differentiating at $t=0$: $R'(0)^T + R'(0) = 0$, so $R'(0)$ is antisymmetric. The Lie algebra is:

$$\mathfrak{so}(2) = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -v \\ v & 0 \end{pmatrix} : v \in \mathbb{R} \right\}$$

The isomorphism $U(1) \cong SO(2)$ sends $iv \mapsto \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-v\\v&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ at the Lie algebra level. Both are 1-dimensional with trivial bracket.

🌀 The Exponential Map

The exponential map $\exp: \mathfrak{g} \to G$ is the bridge between the flat Lie algebra and the curved Lie group. For $U(1)$, it is the complex exponential:

$$\exp(iv) = e^{iv} = \cos v + i\sin v$$

But its meaning runs deeper than a formula: it is the solution to a differential equation, and geometrically it wraps the real line around the circle.

The ODE definition: flowing along a one-parameter subgroup

Given a tangent vector $X \in \mathfrak{g}$ at the identity, there is a unique smooth curve $\gamma: \mathbb{R} \to G$ satisfying:

$$\gamma'(t) = X \cdot \gamma(t), \qquad \gamma(0) = e$$

This curve $\gamma$ is a one-parameter subgroup: it satisfies $\gamma(s+t) = \gamma(s)\cdot\gamma(t)$ — it is a group homomorphism from $(\mathbb{R}, +)$ to $(G, \cdot)$. The exponential map sends $X$ to the group element you reach after unit time:

$$\exp(X) = \gamma(1)$$

For $U(1)$: wrapping the real line

With $X = iv$, the ODE reads $\gamma'(t) = iv \cdot \gamma(t)$, $\gamma(0) = 1$. The solution is $\gamma(t) = e^{ivt}$. As $t$ increases:

  • $t=0$: at the identity $1$
  • $t=1$: at $e^{iv}$ — the exponential map's output
  • $t=2\pi/v$: back at $1$ — one full revolution
  • $t \to \infty$: the point winds around the circle forever

The infinite flat real line (the Lie algebra $i\mathbb{R}$) wraps around the compact circle infinitely many times. The kernel is $\{2\pi i n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ — values of $iv$ for which $e^{iv} = 1$.

Euler's formula as Lie theory

The most beautiful equation in mathematics is an instance of the exponential map for $U(1)$:

$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

Read it as: start at the identity $1 \in U(1)$; flow in the direction $i \in \mathfrak{u}(1)$ for time $\pi$; you arrive at $-1$ (a half-rotation). The five constants aren't mystically related — they are simply the natural landmarks of the wrapping map:

  • $e$: the base of the exponential (the fundamental constant of the exp map)
  • $i$: the generator of $\mathfrak{u}(1)$, the unit tangent at identity
  • $\pi$: the parameter for a half-turn rotation
  • $1$: the identity element of $U(1)$
  • $0$: makes $e^{i\pi} = -1$ look like an additive equation

Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff: the abelian shortcut

For non-abelian groups, $\exp(X)\exp(Y) \neq \exp(X+Y)$ in general. The correction is given by the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff (BCH) formula:

$$\exp(X)\exp(Y) = \exp\!\Bigl(X + Y + \tfrac{1}{2}[X,Y] + \tfrac{1}{12}[X,[X,Y]] - \tfrac{1}{12}[Y,[X,Y]] + \cdots\Bigr)$$

Every correction term involves Lie brackets. For $U(1)$, all brackets vanish, so:

$$\exp(iv_1)\exp(iv_2) = \exp(i(v_1+v_2)) \qquad \text{(since } [iv_1, iv_2] = 0\text{)}$$

This is just angle addition again — $U(1)$ is so simple that BCH collapses to ordinary addition. For $SO(3)$, the non-zero brackets give a genuinely complex formula for composing rotations around different axes.

🌐 Beyond S¹

The circle is just the first rung. The same three ideas — (1) group structure, (2) Lie algebra as tangent space at identity, (3) exponential map — work for all Lie groups. Here we peek at the next examples, which arise from rotating 3-dimensional space.

$SO(3)$: Rotations in 3D

$SO(3)$ is the group of $3\times3$ real rotation matrices — all rotations of 3D space about the origin, with no reflections. It has dimension 3 (one angle for each axis $x, y, z$).

Its Lie algebra is:

$$\mathfrak{so}(3) = \left\{ \Omega \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times3} : \Omega^T = -\Omega \right\}$$

Antisymmetric $3\times3$ matrices have 3 free parameters. A natural basis:

$$L_x = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&{-1}\\0&1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad L_y = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\\0&0&0\\{-1}&0&0\end{pmatrix},\quad L_z = \begin{pmatrix}0&{-1}&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}$$

The Lie brackets are $[L_x, L_y] = L_z$, $[L_y, L_z] = L_x$, $[L_z, L_x] = L_y$ — the cross product on $\mathbb{R}^3$. This is where non-commutativity enters: rotations around different axes don't commute.

The exponential map gives the Rodrigues rotation formula: for axis $\hat{n}$ and angle $\alpha$,

$$\exp(\alpha\,\hat{n}\cdot\mathbf{L}) = I + \sin\alpha\,(\hat{n}\cdot\mathbf{L}) + (1-\cos\alpha)\,(\hat{n}\cdot\mathbf{L})^2$$

This converts an unconstrained vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ (axis-angle) into a valid rotation matrix — the key tool in every robotics and graphics engine.

$SU(2)$: The Double Cover of $SO(3)$

$SU(2)$ is the group of $2\times2$ complex matrices $U$ with $U^\dagger U = I$ and $\det U = 1$:

$$SU(2) = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} \alpha & -\bar\beta \\ \beta & \bar\alpha \end{pmatrix} : |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 \right\}$$

As a manifold, $SU(2) \cong S^3$ (the 3-sphere in $\mathbb{R}^4$). There is a 2-to-1 surjective homomorphism $\pi: SU(2) \to SO(3)$: both $U$ and $-U$ give the same rotation. $SU(2)$ is the universal cover of $SO(3)$.

$SU(2) \cong$ unit quaternions. The quaternion $q = a + b\mathbf{i} + c\mathbf{j} + d\mathbf{k}$ with $|q|=1$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^3$ by conjugation $\mathbf{v} \mapsto q\mathbf{v}q^{-1}$. Since both $q$ and $-q$ give the same rotation, quaternions are the double cover. This is why quantum spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ particles (electrons, quarks) require a $4\pi$ rotation — not $2\pi$ — to return to their original quantum state. The effect has been physically measured in neutron interferometry experiments.

🔬 Applications

$U(1)$ gauge theory = Electromagnetism

In quantum mechanics, the wavefunction $\psi(x)$ has a global phase freedom: $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ doesn't change any observable. But demanding this symmetry hold locally — allowing $\alpha$ to depend on position: $\psi(x) \to e^{i\alpha(x)}\psi(x)$ — forces the introduction of a compensating gauge field $A_\mu$. This compensating field is the electromagnetic potential. Maxwell's equations are the statement that $U(1)$ gauge symmetry is local.

The gauge boson (the photon) is the quantum of the $U(1)$ gauge field. Its masslessness follows from the gauge symmetry. Electromagnetism is Lie theory.

Spinors and the double cover

Because the covering map $SU(2) \to SO(3)$ is 2-to-1, a physical rotation by $2\pi$ takes an electron's state to its negative: $\psi \to -\psi$. This has been directly measured: neutrons travelling through a magnetic field (a $2\pi$ rotation) acquire a phase of $-1$, confirmed by neutron interferometry (Werner et al., 1975). The $U(1)$ phase structure in quantum mechanics and the $SU(2)$ spinor structure are both manifestations of Lie theory.

Fourier analysis = representation theory of $U(1)$

A representation of a group $G$ is a smooth homomorphism $\rho: G \to GL(V)$ for some vector space $V$. For $U(1)$, the 1-dimensional complex representations are exactly the characters:

$$\chi_n: U(1) \to \mathbb{C}^*, \quad e^{i\theta} \mapsto e^{in\theta}, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}$$

The Fourier series of a function $f: S^1 \to \mathbb{C}$ decomposes it into these characters:

$$f(\theta) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(n)\, e^{in\theta}, \qquad \hat{f}(n) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta)\,e^{-in\theta}\,d\theta$$

The orthogonality of the Fourier modes $\langle e^{im\theta}, e^{in\theta}\rangle = \delta_{mn}$ is the Peter–Weyl theorem for $U(1)$: the characters of distinct irreducible representations are orthogonal in $L^2(G)$.

Why equivariance matters in practice

If a molecule is rotated by $R \in SO(3)$, its predicted energy should not change (invariance), and predicted forces should rotate accordingly (equivariance). Without building in this symmetry, a network must learn it from data — requiring vastly more examples. AlphaFold2 uses $SE(3)$-equivariant Invariant Point Attention; each amino acid residue is a rigid body frame, and equivariance guarantees consistent predictions regardless of the global orientation of the input protein.

Optimisation on Lie groups

When network weights live on a Lie group (e.g., orientations in $SO(3)$), the exponential map is the tool for gradient descent. A gradient step in the flat Lie algebra is mapped back to the curved group via $\exp$: if the current weight is $g \in G$ and the gradient descent direction is $\nabla \in \mathfrak{g}$, the update is $g \leftarrow g \cdot \exp(-\eta\nabla)$. Riemannian batch normalisation (Zhu et al., ICLR 2024) applies this idea to normalise group-valued features layer by layer.

Rigid Body Motion: $SE(3)$

A robot's pose (position + orientation) in 3D space is an element of the Special Euclidean group:

$$SE(3) = SO(3) \ltimes \mathbb{R}^3 = \left\{ T = \begin{pmatrix} R & \mathbf{t} \\ \mathbf{0}^T & 1 \end{pmatrix} : R \in SO(3),\; \mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^3 \right\}$$

Group multiplication is pose composition; the inverse is the reverse motion. The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{se}(3)$ is 6-dimensional: angular velocity (3 components) + linear velocity (3 components) = a screw.

Why not just use Euler angles?

Euler angles (three nested angles $\phi, \theta, \psi$) suffer from gimbal lock: at certain configurations, two axes align and you lose a degree of freedom — the Jacobian becomes singular. Rotation matrices have 6 redundant constraints. The Lie algebra approach avoids both problems: the axis-angle representation $\boldsymbol{\omega} \in \mathbb{R}^3 \cong \mathfrak{so}(3)$ has no singularities, and $\exp$ always produces a valid rotation.

The Product of Exponentials formula is the Lie-theoretic replacement for Denavit–Hartenberg parameters. For a robot arm with $n$ joints, the forward kinematics is: $T_\text{end}(\boldsymbol\theta) = e^{\hat{\xi}_1\theta_1} \cdots e^{\hat{\xi}_n\theta_n} \cdot T_0$ where each $\hat{\xi}_k \in \mathfrak{se}(3)$ is the joint's screw axis. Changing any one joint angle $\theta_k$ simply changes one exponential — clean and compositional.
