About the Song

Artist: Zild Benitez
Album: Medisina (October 2022)
Label: Island Records Philippines / UMG

Title meaning: “Bahala Ka Na” is a Filipino phrase meaning “it’s up to you now” — an expression of resigned acceptance, of letting go and releasing control.

Zild (Daniel Zildjian Benitez) is a Filipino multi-instrumentalist, singer-songwriter, and self-producer from Quezon City. He rose to fame as the bassist of IV of Spades, a funk-rock band with a 1970s disco-soul aesthetic. His solo career took a sharp turn into introspective, piano-driven territory: Medisina draws on post-punk and new wave influences (Joy Division, Radiohead, The 1975) while keeping his instinct for melodic bass thinking.

“Bahala Ka Na” is a slow, piano-led breakup song. The lyrics address an ex-partner directly — asking one last thing, acknowledging the distance — with a tone of melancholy but resolve. It fits perfectly with its harmonic language: resigned, floating, never quite resolving.

What This Explainer Covers

This page uses “Bahala Ka Na” as a gateway into three interconnected music theory ideas:

  1. The Aeolian mode (natural minor) and what “no leading tone” feels like in practice
  2. A VI–iv–v chord loop and why the minor dominant (v) keeps the harmony floating
  3. Extended chords — specifically Amaj7, and how a major seventh chord can blur the line between major and minor

Each concept is illustrated with the actual Strudel code from the song, which you can play and edit in the final section.

Learn by doing. The last section embeds a live Strudel editor. After reading the theory, you can modify the code — swap G#m for G# major, change Amaj7 to a plain A chord — and hear what the theory predicts.

Key & Scale

C♯ Natural Minor (Aeolian Mode)

Every note in “Bahala Ka Na” comes from a single seven-note pool. If you collect all the notes in the Strudel program — A, B, C♯, D♯, E, F♯, G♯ — they spell out the C♯ natural minor scale, also called C♯ Aeolian.

What Is Aeolian?

The Aeolian mode is simply the natural minor scale. “Natural” means it uses the scale degrees exactly as they appear — no sharps or flats added beyond the key signature. The most important consequence is what it lacks: a raised 7th degree.

The 7th stays flat (B♮). No leading tone — no urgency. The harmony floats.

The leading tone is what makes music “want to resolve.” When the 7th is raised a half step (B♯/C♮ in C♯ minor), it sits one semitone below the tonic and creates strong upward pull. Aeolian keeps it at B♮ — a whole step away — so there’s no magnetic pull home. The progression can loop forever without feeling incomplete.

Relative Major: E Major

C♯ natural minor is the relative minor of E major — they share the same seven notes. The difference is only which note functions as “home.” In “Bahala Ka Na,” the chord progression confirms C♯ minor as the tonal center, not E major — the song opens and returns to C♯ minor-flavored harmony.

In Strudel: You could rewrite the melody using scale degrees instead of raw note names:
n("5 0 5 4@2 2 0 2").scale("C#4:minor").sound("piano")
Here 0 = C♯, 2 = E, 4 = G♯, 5 = A — the same Amaj7 arpeggio pattern.

The Chord Loop

Amaj7 – F♯m – G♯m, repeated through every section

“Bahala Ka Na” is built on a single three-chord progression that repeats unchanged through the intro, verse, pre-chorus, chorus, and outro. There is no bridge with contrasting harmony, no key change, no moment of harmonic surprise. The emotional arc of the song lives entirely in the melody, dynamics, and production — not in chord movement.

Why It Floats

Two harmonic details that create the song’s bittersweet, unresolved quality

You now know the key (C♯ Aeolian) and the chords (Amaj7–F♯m–G♯m). Two specific harmonic choices make this progression feel the way it does: resigned, floating, neither fully dark nor fully bright. Both are deliberate consequences of staying in natural minor.

1. The Minor Dominant: G♯m Instead of G♯ Major

In most minor-key music, the chord built on the 5th scale degree — the dominant — is major. This requires raising the 7th of the scale (a technique called harmonic minor). The result is a major V chord with a leading tone that strongly pulls back to the tonic.

G♯ major (V) — harmonic minor

G♯   B♯   D♯

B♯ (= C♮) is one semitone below C♯. It wants to resolve upward to the tonic. Strong cadential pull. Sounds like: “and we’re home.”

G♯m (v) — natural minor / Aeolian

G♯   B♮   D♯

B♮ is a whole step below C♯. No leading tone, no urgency. The chord lands and … just rests there. Sounds like: “oh well.”

This is the harmonic signature of the song. Every time the progression reaches G♯m, it should feel like a moment of potential resolution — but it stays minor, and the loop begins again. This perfectly mirrors “bahala ka na”: the moment of letting go that never quite arrives as closure.

You can test this directly in Strudel. In the Listen section, try replacing G#3 B3 D#4 with G#3 B#3 D#4 (B♯ = C♮) in both G♯m lines and hear how it changes from floating to resolving.

2. Amaj7: The Chord That Blurs Major and Minor

The progression opens — and keeps returning to — Amaj7. Let’s look at its notes:

A    C♯    E    G♯

  • A — root of the chord; also the ♭6 of C♯ minor
  • C♯ — the tonic of the key, embedded inside a major chord
  • E — the ♭3 of C♯ minor; the note that makes the key minor
  • G♯ — the 5th of C♯ minor; and the major 7th of Amaj7

Notice: C♯ (the tonic) and G♯ (the 5th of the tonic) are both present inside Amaj7. When you hear this chord, the two most important notes of C♯ minor are sounding — but they’re housed inside a major chord. The result is a sound that simultaneously evokes C♯ minor and A major. The chord feels warm and bright (major quality) while being harmonically rooted in a minor key.

The maj7 color. The major-seventh interval (G♯ above A) is what distinguishes Amaj7 from a plain A major chord. It adds a shimmer, a slightly dreamy quality. Without it, A major is bright and direct. With the G♯, it becomes bittersweet — which is exactly the emotional register of “Bahala Ka Na.”

Together: The Full Emotional Picture

Put the two details together:

  • Amaj7 — bittersweet, blurs major/minor, warm but ungrounded
  • F♯m — clearly minor, a moment of darker color
  • G♯m — should resolve but doesn’t, the loop restarts

The progression creates a mood of suspended acceptance — not grief, not happiness, just the sustained emotional state of having let go of something. It matches the Filipino idiom “bahala na” at the level of harmony: leave it to fate, stop holding on, let the cycle repeat.

The Strudel Code

Line-by-line: what each part does and why

Strudel is a browser-based live coding music tool — a JavaScript port of TidalCycles. Music is organized around cycles (repeating time loops) rather than bars. The code below creates three simultaneous patterns that run in sync.

setcpm(5)
$: sound("[bd sd]*8")
$: note(`
A3 C#4 A3 G#4@2 E4 C#4 E4
F#3 A3 C#4 G#4@2 E4 C#4 E4
G#3 B3 D#4 F#4@2 D#4 B3 D#4
G#3 C4 D#4 F#4@2 D#4 C4 D#4
`).sound("piano")

$: note(`
C#5 A4 E4 - -!4
-!8
B4 G#4 D#4 - -!4
-!8
`).sound("piano").gain(3)

Annotation

setcpm(5)
Tempo: 5 cycles per minute. Strudel measures time in cycles (one full loop of a pattern). At 5 CPM, each cycle lasts 12 seconds. The drum pattern has 16 hits per cycle — so the effective pulse is about 80 BPM, which matches the song’s slow, contemplative pace.
$: sound("[bd sd]*8")
Drums: alternating kick and snare, 8 times per cycle.
  • $: marks this as one of several simultaneous patterns
  • [bd sd] — a two-step sequence: bass drum, then snare
  • *8 — repeat the sequence 8 times per cycle
  • Result: 16 total hits per cycle in a steady kick-snare-kick-snare pulse
note(`…`).sound("piano")
Chord arpeggiation: the harmonic backbone. The four lines inside the backtick string each last one cycle, so the complete pattern takes 4 cycles before looping. Each line outlines a chord from the progression:
LinePatternChord it outlines
1A3 C#4 A3 G#4@2 E4 C#4 E4Amaj7 — A, C♯, E, G♯
2F#3 A3 C#4 G#4@2 E4 C#4 E4F♯m — F♯, A, C♯
3G#3 B3 D#4 F#4@2 D#4 B3 D#4G♯m — G♯, B, D♯
4G#3 C4 D#4 F#4@2 D#4 C4 D#4G♯m variant (C4 = chromatic color)

The @2 on G♯4 means it lasts twice as long as the surrounding notes — creating a sustained mid-pattern emphasis.

note(`…`).sound("piano").gain(3)
Melody: sparse chord tones at a higher register. This layer is mostly rests, with brief chordal stabs at the top:
  • C#5 A4 E4 — C♯ minor triad in the upper register (C♯ E A)
  • - — a single rest step
  • -!4 — four rest steps (“!” = replicate: four silent slots)
  • -!8 — eight rests = a full silent cycle
  • B4 G#4 D#4 — G♯m/B voicing (B at the bottom)
  • .gain(3) — amplifies this voice 3× so the melody sits above the arpeggiation

The silences give the pattern breathing room. Each chord stab rings briefly, then the arpeggiation carries the harmony forward alone.

Things to Try

  • Minor v → major V: Change B3 to B#3 on the G♯m lines. B♯ is C♮ — the leading tone — which turns G♯m into G♯ major. Hear how it resolves instead of floating.
  • Remove the maj7: Change G#4@2 on line 1 to E4@2. This drops the major 7th from Amaj7, making it a plainer A major chord.
  • Speed up: Change setcpm(5) to setcpm(15) or setcpm(30). Same chords, different energy.
  • Add reverb: Add .room(0.6) before .sound("piano") on either note voice.
  • Scale degrees: Replace the first note line with n("5 0 5 4@2 2 0 2").scale("C#4:minor") and drop the .sound("piano") onto that line.
