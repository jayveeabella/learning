Soldier On

The Temper Trap — Conditions (2009) — a learn-by-doing music theory explainer

About the Song

Artist: The Temper Trap
Album: Conditions (2009)
Label: Mercury / Island Records
Written by: Dougy Mandagi, Jonathon Aherne, Lorenzo Sillitto

Title meaning: “Soldier On” means to press forward despite hardship — to endure, push through pain, and keep moving. It captures a steely, aching perseverance.

The Temper Trap are an Australian indie rock band from Melbourne, formed around 2005. Their lineup: Dougy Mandagi (vocals, percussion), Lorenzo Sillitto (guitar), Jonathon Aherne (bass), and Toby Dundas (drums). Mandagi was born in Indonesia and moved to Melbourne, bringing an intensity and vulnerability to his voice that drives much of the band’s emotional power.

Conditions was their debut full-length album, and it broke through internationally on the back of “Sweet Disposition.” “Soldier On” is a slower, more introspective moment on the record — a patient, winding track built on acoustic guitar arpeggios that never quite resolves the tension it sets up.

What This Explainer Covers

This page uses the intro of “Soldier On” as a gateway into three connected music theory ideas:

  1. F♯ natural minor (Aeolian) — the governing scale and what it means for chord choices
  2. The iv–VI–i–♭II chord loop — an unusual progression with one chord that doesn’t belong to the key
  3. The Neapolitan chord (♭II) — the G major chord sitting a half step above F♯, borrowed from outside the scale, and what it does emotionally

The Neapolitan is the heart of this explainer. It’s the chord that makes the intro feel tense and slightly unsettled — like someone bracing themselves before the next step. Perfect for a song called “Soldier On.”

Learn by doing. The last section embeds a live Strudel editor. After reading the theory, you can modify the code — swap the Neapolitan for a more conventional chord, strip the “add” tones, change the tempo — and hear what the theory predicts.

Key & Scale

F♯ Natural Minor (Aeolian Mode)

The Strudel program uses .scale("F#:minor"), which locks every note to the F♯ natural minor scale — also called F♯ Aeolian. Seven notes, cycling through octaves:

Root highlighted in gold. The ♭ symbols indicate minor (flattened) intervals relative to F♯ major.

Notice what’s in the scale: G♯ (degree 2). The natural 2nd in F♯ minor is G♯, not G♮. This matters: the chord progression borrows a G♮ major chord — a note outside the scale. That’s the Neapolitan, and it’s the source of the intro’s characteristic tension.

Relative Major: A Major

F♯ natural minor is the relative minor of A major — they share the same seven notes (F♯, G♯, A, B, C♯, D, E). The difference is which note acts as “home.” In “Soldier On,” the progression anchors on F♯m (the i chord), making F♯ the tonal center.

What Aeolian Means Here

The Aeolian mode is natural minor — no alterations. The key consequence is that the chord on the 5th scale degree (C♯) is minor, not major. There is no leading tone (no B♯ pulling up to C♯), so the harmony doesn’t have a strong cadential pull toward resolution. Things float, cycle, endure. This matches the song’s theme of grinding persistence.

In Strudel: Scale degrees in .scale("F#:minor"):
0=F♯   1=G♯   2=A   3=B   4=C♯   5=D   6=E   7=F♯(oct+)
Negative degrees go below the register: -1=E   -2=D. The suffix b flattens by one semitone: 1b = G♯ − 1 = G♮.

The Chord Loop

Bm – Dadd9 – F♯m – Gadd6, cycling through the intro

The intro of “Soldier On” loops a four-chord progression on arpeggiated acoustic guitar. Three of the four chords are diatonic to F♯ natural minor; the fourth — G major — is not. That borrowed chord is what gives the intro its slightly off-kilter, yearning character.

The shared note E. Both Dadd9 and Gadd6 include E — the ♭7 of F♯ minor. This creates a thread of continuity across the two middle chords. Even as the bass moves D → F♯ → G, E keeps ringing as a color note, giving the progression a sense of hovering.

The Progression in Context

The order iv → VI → i → ♭II creates a specific arc: begin in darkness (Bm), open up toward the relative major (Dadd9), briefly arrive home (F♯m), then be pulled sideways by the Neapolitan (Gadd6) before cycling back. The loop never fully settles — every arrival at F♯m is immediately followed by the disruptive G♮ chord. You reach home, and then something yanks at you again. Soldier on.

The Neapolitan

The chord that doesn’t belong — and why it works

The most interesting harmonic moment in this intro is the last chord of each loop: Gadd6. Let’s understand why it’s so striking.

1. Why G Major Doesn’t Belong

F♯ natural minor contains the note G♯, not G♮. The chord naturally built on the 2nd scale degree is G♯ diminished (G♯, B, D) — a tense, unstable chord. G major is a completely different chord using a chromatic note (G♮) that doesn’t appear in the scale at all.

G♯° (ii°) — diatonic

G♯2
B4
D♭6

The natural ii°: diminished quality, unstable, dissonant. Rarely used as a resting point in rock.

G major (♭II) — borrowed

G♮♭2
B4
D♭6

G♮ is one semitone above F♯. The chord is major, stable, and sonically powerful as a result. Borrowed from outside the scale.

The ♭II chord is called the Neapolitan. In classical harmony, it’s named after the Neapolitan school of opera, where this chromatic chord was used for emotional intensity before a cadence. In modern rock and pop, it’s used more freely — not always as a pre-cadential device, but simply as a chromatic color that injects urgency.

2. The Half-Step Pull

The Neapolitan’s power comes from a simple interval: the bass note G♮ is exactly one semitone above F♯. This is the closest two notes can be in Western music. When the harmony moves G♮ → F♯ (which it does every time the loop cycles back to Bm), the ear hears a very tight, almost gravitational pull downward.

G♮  →  F♯

One semitone downward — the smallest possible step. This is called the Phrygian half-step, and it’s the signature sound of the Neapolitan chord resolving to the tonic.

Compare this to the normal 2nd-degree chord in major keys: there, the bass moves a whole step down (e.g., G to F in C major). The half-step pull of the Neapolitan is more intense — tighter, more insistent. It suits a song about forcing yourself to keep going.

The Phrygian connection. The Phrygian mode (1, ♭2, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭6, ♭7) has the same lowered second degree. Music in Phrygian mode — flamenco, metal, certain film scores — uses ♭II as its central color chord. “Soldier On” isn’t in Phrygian mode, but it borrows the ♭II chord and with it that same flavor: dark, intense, slightly Spanish or epic. It’s one chord, used once per loop, but it colors the entire progression.

3. The “Add” Extensions: Shared Color

Two of the four chords have added tones: Dadd9 and Gadd6. Both add the same note:

Dadd9: D   F♯   A   E
Gadd6: G   B   D   E

  • In Dadd9, E is the 9th (= 2nd above D, which is E). It’s the ♭7 of F♯ minor.
  • In Gadd6, E is the 6th (= 6th above G). Same note E — same function as a linking color tone.
  • E is not in the root triad of either chord — it’s an extra note added for texture.

The recurring E creates timbral continuity across the loop: even as the bass and chord quality shift dramatically (D major → G major), the note E hangs in the harmony as a connective thread. It gives the progression a slightly open, unresolved shimmer even on the chords that would otherwise feel settled.

Putting It All Together

  • Bm (iv) — dark and stable; the song’s emotional ground floor
  • Dadd9 (VI) — brightens toward relative major; E adds shimmer
  • F♯m (i) — the tonic arrives, but doesn’t stay
  • Gadd6 (♭II) — the Neapolitan wrenches the harmony sideways; G♮ creates half-step pull; E links back to Dadd9

The loop never gets to rest. Every arrival at F♯m is undercut by the Neapolitan. This harmonic restlessness is the music’s way of enacting the title: there is no resolution, only the act of continuing.

The Strudel Code

Five stacked voices modeling an acoustic guitar arpeggio

Strudel is a browser-based live coding tool where music is organized around cycles (repeating time loops). The code below stacks five simultaneous note patterns, all using the same gm_acoustic_guitar_steel sound, to recreate the layered arpeggiated guitar texture of the intro.

setcpm(5)
// -2 to get A:major
$: stack(
  note("3*4 -2*4 0*4 1b*4"),
  note("5*4 7*4 2*4 3*4").late(1/32),
  note("[<[7, 10] [7, 10] [6, 9] [6, 9] [4, 7] [4, 7] [5, 6] [5, 6]> -]*8").late(1/32),
  note("[- <7 10 6 9 4 7 5 6>]*8").late(1/64),
  note("[- <10 7 9 6 7 4 6 5>]*8").late(3/64),
).sound("gm_acoustic_guitar_steel").scale("F#:minor")

Annotation

Tempo: 5 cycles per minute. One cycle = 12 seconds. This is very slow, which suits the introspective, patient character of the intro. Each chord in voices 1 and 2 occupies one-quarter of a cycle (3 seconds), giving the arpeggios space to breathe.

Upper harmony: a second note above each bass note, delayed slightly.
DegreeNoteOver chord
5DB+D = Bm (B D F♯)

The .late(1/32) delays this voice by 1/32 of a cycle, creating the rolling strum effect of a guitar: bass string first, then the upper strings follow.
note("[<[7,10]…[5,6]> -]*8").late(1/32)
Inner chord tones: pairs of notes cycling over 8 passes per cycle. The <> syntax advances one step each cycle, so each chord pair plays for two full cycles before moving on. The pairs are:
  • [7, 10] = F♯ + B (cycles 1–2) → completes the Bm triad
  • [6, 9] = E + A (cycles 3–4) → the add9 (E) in Dadd9
  • [4, 7] = C♯ + F♯ (cycles 5–6) → completes F♯m
  • [5, 6] = D + E (cycles 7–8) → the add6 (E) in Gadd6
The rest (-) between each pair gives the notes rhythmic breathing room.
note("[- <7 10 6 9 4 7 5 6>]*8").late(1/64)
Upper arpeggiated voice, single notes. Plays one scale degree at a time, cycling through chord tones: F♯, B (Bm) → E, A (Dadd9) → C♯, F♯ (F♯m) → E, D (Gadd6). Each note is preceded by a rest (-), so it’s sparse and intermittent. Delayed by 1/64, it sits slightly behind the chord-tone pairs.
note("[- <10 7 9 6 7 4 6 5>]*8").late(3/64)
Highest arpeggiated voice, offset further. The same chord tones as voice 4 but in reverse order (descending): B, F♯ (Bm) → A, E (Dadd9) → F♯, C♯ (F♯m) → E, D (Gadd6). The 3/64 delay places it last in the strum cascade, giving the impression of a guitar’s top strings landing after the lower ones.
.sound("gm_acoustic_guitar_steel").scale("F#:minor")
Timbre and scale context, shared by all five voices. gm_acoustic_guitar_steel uses the General MIDI acoustic steel-string guitar sample bank, giving a realistic plucked string texture. .scale("F#:minor") maps all integer degree values (0, 1, 2…) onto notes of the F♯ natural minor scale, so you write scale steps rather than raw note names.

Key Strudel Syntax

SyntaxMeaning
$: stack(...)Run all listed patterns simultaneously (one layer)
*4Repeat the preceding event 4 times per cycle
1bScale degree 1, flattened (lowered one semitone)
-2Scale degree 2 below the default octave
<a b c>Alternate: play a on cycle 1, b on cycle 2, c on cycle 3…
[7, 10]Play degrees 7 and 10 simultaneously (a two-note chord)
*8Repeat the pattern 8 times per cycle
.late(1/32)Delay by 1/32 of a cycle — creates the rolling strum effect
The staggered delays are doing the real work. Voices 1–5 are all the same sound, but they arrive at 0, 1/32, 1/32, 1/64, 3/64 of a cycle. This tiny cascade mimics the natural roll of a guitar chord: the low strings hit first, the high strings follow milliseconds later. Without the .late() calls, all five voices would sound simultaneously as a hard strum.

Things to Try

  • Remove the Neapolitan — swap ♭II for ♭VII: Change 1b*4 to 6*4 in voice 1. The bass note shifts from G♮ (♭II) to E (♭VII). The ♭VII (E major) is a conventional rock choice in minor keys. Notice how the exotic, wrenching half-step pull disappears and the loop becomes smoother.
  • Hear the Phrygian half-step in isolation: Change note("3*4 -2*4 0*4 1b*4") to note("0*4 0*4 0*4 1b*4") to repeat the tonic three times before hitting the Neapolitan. The contrast between F♯ and the G♮ that follows becomes stark — you can really hear that half-step pull.
  • Strip the add9 from Dadd9: In voice 3, change [6, 9] to [5, 9]. This replaces E with D in the inner chord, removing the add9 extension. Plain D major vs Dadd9 — hear the shimmer disappear.
  • Try piano instead of guitar: Change .sound("gm_acoustic_guitar_steel") to .sound("piano"). Same notes, very different texture — and the staggered .late() delays feel different on a sustained instrument.
  • Speed it up: Change setcpm(5) to setcpm(15). Same harmonic content, but the increased pace shifts the feel from meditative to urgent.
